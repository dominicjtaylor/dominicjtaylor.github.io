ISM at Cosmic Noon

An investigation into the molecular gas properties of dusty, star-forming galaxies

Introduction

Cosmological context

Molecular gas plays a key role in galaxy evolution, providing the raw fuel for star formation. Observations have shown that the molecular gas fraction in galaxies evolves strongly with redshift, increasing up to ~50% for massive galaxies at high redshift (z~2).

Submillimetre Galaxies

Submillimetre galaxies (SMGs) found at high redshifts have high star-formation rates and dust masses similar to local ultra-luminous infrared galaxies (ULIRGs) and are therefore ideal candidates for investigating star-formation in the early Universe. The carbon monoxide (12CO) emission is commonly used as a powerful diagnostic of the properties of the molecular hydrogen. Furthermore, the diversity of the spectral line energy distributions (SLEDs) of CO is expected to reflect the gas density and temperature of the interstellar medium, which in turn is related to the properties of the star-forming regions. Recent findings from hydrodynamical simulations have suggested that the shapes of CO SLEDs should strongly correlate with the galaxy-averaged star-formation rate surface densities.

For this investigation, I analysed the carbon monoxide (CO) spectral line energy distributions (SLEDs) in a sample of twelve dusty, star-forming galaxies at high redshifts (z ~ 2–4) using observations from ALMA and JVLA. The study investigates the relationship between the shape of these CO SLEDs and various physical properties of the galaxies, such as star-formation efficiency, radiation field intensity, and crucially, star-formation rate surface density (ΣSFR). The findings suggest that ΣSFR is the primary driver of high-J CO excitation in these galaxies, and the observed SLED shapes are generally consistent with theoretical models, although some scatter indicates potential influence from other factors within the interstellar medium.

The findings can be summarised as follows:
  • The CO(6-5) line luminosity of the studied SMGs is consistent with other high-redshift SMGs.
  • The typical CO SLED of these SMGs shows higher excitation than the Milky Way and is comparable to local starbursts and ULIRGs.
  • The strongest driver of high-Jup CO excitation in star-forming galaxies, when considering a broad redshift range, is the star-formation rate surface density (ΣSFR), highlighting the importance of galaxy compactness.
  • While the Narayanan & Krumholz (2014) model provides a generally consistent picture, significant scatter in the observed CO excitation suggests the influence of other factors, such as cosmic ray ionization rates and turbulence dissipation.
  • Future studies with multi-Jup 13CO observations and higher spatial resolution will be crucial for further constraining ISM properties and refining theoretical models.
